\section*{Purpose and Main Question}

This report records a diagnostic framework for understanding why the local
chart-dimension version of the local polynomial smoother (LPS) differs from the
global chart-dimension version.  The immediate empirical question is not only
whether
\[
  \texttt{chart.dim = "local.auto"}
\]
has lower Truth RMSE than
\[
  \texttt{chart.dim = "auto"}.
\]
The deeper question is \emph{which data points and which local-dimension
decisions drive the dataset-level difference}.

The paired HTML report showed the overall Truth-RMSE difference between the two
rules.  Such a number is useful, but it is too compressed.  A small global
difference could mean that nearly every point changed a little, or it could
mean that a few high-impact points changed a lot while most points were
irrelevant.  It could also mean that local dimension one helped in one region
while local dimension three hurt in another.  This report defines five
complementary meanings of a pointwise or grouped ``component'' of the
Truth-RMSE delta, then applies them to the first dataset in the current HTML
report:
\[
  \texttt{FB01. LA-D1-RAW-N500}.
\]

\paragraph{Executive summary for FB01.}
For this dataset, the global-auto fit used chart dimension
\(\fbAutoDim\) everywhere, selected support size \(\fbAutoSupport\), Gaussian
kernel, and had Truth RMSE \(\fbAutoTruthRmse\).  The local-auto fit selected
support size \(\fbLocalSupport\), Gaussian kernel, and used pointwise local
chart dimensions \(1,2,3\) with counts
\(\fbDimOneCount,\fbDimTwoCount,\fbDimThreeCount\), respectively.  Its Truth
RMSE was \(\fbLocalTruthRmse\).  Therefore local-auto was worse by
\[
  \fbDeltaTruthRmse.
\]
The exact pointwise decomposition below sums to
\[
  \fbComponentSum,
\]
which matches the observed Truth-RMSE delta up to displayed rounding.

\section{Definitions}

Let \(n\) be the number of points in a synthetic dataset.  At point \(i\), let
\(f_i\) be the true signal and \(y_i\) be the noisy observation:
\[
  y_i = f_i + \varepsilon_i,
  \qquad i=1,\ldots,n.
\]
Let \(\widehat f_i^{a}\) be the fitted value from the global-auto chart
dimension rule and let \(\widehat f_i^{la}\) be the fitted value from the
local-auto chart dimension rule.  The corresponding truth errors are
\[
  e_i^{a} = \widehat f_i^{a} - f_i,
  \qquad
  e_i^{la} = \widehat f_i^{la} - f_i.
\]
The two Truth RMSE values are
\[
  R_a =
  \left\{
    \frac{1}{n}
    \sum_{i=1}^{n}
    (e_i^a)^2
  \right\}^{1/2},
  \qquad
  R_{la} =
  \left\{
    \frac{1}{n}
    \sum_{i=1}^{n}
    (e_i^{la})^2
  \right\}^{1/2}.
\]
The dataset-level Truth-RMSE delta is
\[
  \Delta_{\mathrm{RMSE}}
  =
  R_{la} - R_a.
\]
Positive values mean that local-auto is worse; negative values mean that
local-auto is better.

Let \(d_i^{la}\) be the local-auto chart dimension estimated at point \(i\), and
let \(d^a\) be the single global-auto dimension.  The dimension deviation is
\[
  q_i = d_i^{la} - d^a.
\]
In FB01, \(d^a=\fbAutoDim\), while \(d_i^{la}\) takes values 1, 2, and 3.

\section{Five Ways to Interpret a Pointwise or Grouped Component}

The word ``component'' can mean several related but different things.  The
following five definitions are deliberately complementary.  None by itself
answers every question; together they separate algebraic contribution,
pointwise error geometry, spatial localization, concentration of influence, and
group-level interpretation.

\subsection{Component 1: Exact Pointwise RMSE-Delta Contribution}

Define
\[
  c_i
  =
  \frac{
    (e_i^{la})^2 - (e_i^a)^2
  }{
    n(R_{la}+R_a)
  },
  \qquad R_{la}+R_a>0.
\]
Then
\[
  \Delta_{\mathrm{RMSE}}
  =
  \sum_{i=1}^{n} c_i.
\]
Thus \(c_i\) is an exact additive pointwise contribution to the RMSE
difference.  If \(c_i<0\), point \(i\) helps local-auto.  If \(c_i>0\), point
\(i\) hurts local-auto.

This is the cleanest answer to the vague phrase ``the component of the
dataset-level Truth-RMSE delta.''  It is also the quantity used in most figures
below.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.84\linewidth]{fb01_contribution_by_dimension.png}
  \caption{Pointwise RMSE-delta contributions by dimension deviation for FB01.
  The global-auto chart dimension is \(d^a=\fbAutoDim\).  The horizontal axis is
  \(q_i=d_i^{la}-d^a\).  Negative vertical values are points where local-auto
  improves Truth RMSE relative to auto; positive values are points where
  local-auto worsens Truth RMSE.}
  \label{fig:component-by-dimension}
\end{figure}

For FB01, the dimension-one points slightly help local-auto in aggregate, but
the dimension-two and dimension-three points hurt it in aggregate.  This already
suggests that the worse global Truth RMSE for local-auto is not simply caused
by all dimension changes.  The direction and magnitude depend on which local
dimension was selected and where.

\subsection{Component 2: Pointwise Error-Pair Geometry}

The exact component \(c_i\) is additive, but it hides the sign of the raw errors.
A second diagnostic is the error-pair plot:
\[
  x_i=e_i^a,
  \qquad
  y_i=e_i^{la}.
\]
The point \(i\) is improved by local-auto exactly when
\[
  |e_i^{la}| < |e_i^a|.
\]
Equivalently, the point lies closer to the horizontal axis in the local-auto
coordinate than in the auto coordinate.  The lines
\[
  y=x
  \qquad\text{and}\qquad
  y=-x
\]
mark the boundaries where the two squared errors are equal.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{fb01_error_error_scatter.png}
  \caption{Truth-error scatter for FB01.  The horizontal coordinate is the
  auto truth error \(e_i^a\), and the vertical coordinate is the local-auto
  truth error \(e_i^{la}\).  Points between the two diagonal boundary lines have
  smaller absolute truth error under local-auto than under auto.  Color shows
  the local-auto dimension deviation from the global-auto dimension.}
  \label{fig:error-error}
\end{figure}

This plot asks a different question from Figure~\ref{fig:component-by-dimension}.
Instead of asking how much each point contributes to the final delta, it asks
whether local-auto changes the \emph{error direction and magnitude}.  It can
reveal systematic bias changes, for example a model that tends to overshoot in
one chart-dimension regime and undershoot in another.

\subsection{Component 3: Spatial or Geometric Localization}

A third interpretation is geometric.  The same exact contribution \(c_i\) can be
displayed over a representation of the observed state space.  For FB01, the
observed coordinate matrix is the four-component VALENCIA depth-1 dCST
relative-abundance matrix.  The figure below uses a two-dimensional PCA view of
that four-dimensional coordinate matrix.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{fb01_geometry_contribution_pca.png}
  \caption{Geometry view for FB01, colored by the exact pointwise RMSE-delta
  contribution \(c_i\).  Blue points help local-auto, and red points hurt
  local-auto.  The plot uses the first two principal components of the observed
  four-component relative-abundance coordinates, only as a visualization device.}
  \label{fig:geometry-contribution}
\end{figure}

This view answers whether the delta is spatially localized.  If red and blue
regions form coherent patches, then the local-dimension rule is interacting
with geometry.  If the colors are scattered without structure, then the
difference may be more noise-like or driven by individual leverage points.

\subsection{Component 4: Cumulative Influence or Leverage}

A fourth interpretation asks whether the dataset-level delta is concentrated in
a small number of points.  Sort points by decreasing absolute contribution:
\[
  |c_{(1)}| \ge |c_{(2)}| \ge \cdots \ge |c_{(n)}|.
\]
Then plot
\[
  S_m
  =
  \sum_{r=1}^{m} c_{(r)},
  \qquad m=1,\ldots,n.
\]
If the curve reaches most of the final delta quickly, a small number of points
dominate the comparison.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.84\linewidth]{fb01_cumulative_contribution.png}
  \caption{Cumulative contribution for FB01 after sorting points by decreasing
  absolute pointwise contribution.  The dashed horizontal line is the final
  dataset-level Truth-RMSE delta.  A steep initial curve indicates that a small
  subset of points has high leverage for the local-auto versus auto comparison.}
  \label{fig:cumulative}
\end{figure}

For FB01, the ten largest absolute point contributions account for
approximately \(\fbTopTenAbsPct\%\) of the final signed delta, and the twenty
largest account for approximately \(\fbTopTwentyAbsPct\%\).  This suggests that
the observed difference is not purely diffuse.  A relatively small collection
of points contributes substantially to the final comparison.

\subsection{Component 5: Grouped or Regional Contribution}

Finally, pointwise components can be aggregated over a group \(g\).  Let
\(G_g\) be a subset of point indices, for example all points with a given local
dimension, all points in a biological component label, or all points in a
synthetic stratum.  Define
\[
  C_g
  =
  \sum_{i\in G_g} c_i.
\]
The group contributions add to the same global delta:
\[
  \Delta_{\mathrm{RMSE}}
  =
  \sum_g C_g,
\]
provided the groups form a partition of the dataset.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.84\linewidth]{fb01_grouped_contribution_by_dimension.png}
  \caption{Grouped contribution by local-auto selected dimension for FB01.  Each
  point is the sum of exact pointwise contributions over all data points with a
  given local dimension.  Group sums add to the full Truth-RMSE delta.}
  \label{fig:grouped-dim}
\end{figure}

The grouped decomposition for FB01 is:
\[
\begin{array}{ccl}
  d_i^{la}=1 &:& -0.000164\quad (40\text{ points}),\\
  d_i^{la}=2 &:& \phantom{-}0.000648\quad (410\text{ points}),\\
  d_i^{la}=3 &:& \phantom{-}0.001906\quad (50\text{ points}).
\end{array}
\]
Thus, in this example, points where local-auto reduced dimension below the
global-auto dimension helped slightly.  Points where local-auto matched the
global dimension hurt modestly, and points where local-auto increased dimension
to three hurt most in aggregate.

\section{Interpretation for FB01}

The five diagnostics give a coherent first read of FB01.  The global-auto model
used a single chart dimension of \(\fbAutoDim\).  The local-auto model mostly
agreed with this dimension, but it selected dimension one at \(\fbDimOneCount\)
points and dimension three at \(\fbDimThreeCount\) points.  The net result was a
small worsening of Truth RMSE:
\[
  R_{la}-R_a
  =
  \fbDeltaTruthRmse.
\]

The most informative feature is not simply that local-auto changed dimensions.
Rather, different changes had different consequences.  Dimension-one selections
were slightly beneficial in aggregate.  Dimension-three selections were
unfavorable in aggregate.  This pattern suggests a useful next diagnostic:
inspect whether the dimension-three points correspond to genuinely
higher-dimensional local neighborhoods or to unstable local dimension
overestimation near sparse, noisy, or boundary-heavy regions.

The cumulative contribution plot also says that the comparison is somewhat
high-leverage.  A small number of points explains a large part of the signed
delta.  Therefore, before concluding that local-auto is globally worse on this
kind of dataset, it would be useful to identify these points in the original
coordinate space and examine their local neighborhoods, selected supports, and
fold behavior.

\section{Recommended Use in the HTML Report}

The HTML report should not include every diagnostic for every dataset at full
detail, because that would become visually heavy.  A practical compromise is:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Define the exact contribution \(c_i\) once.
  \item For each dataset, show the contribution-versus-dimension plot and the
  grouped contribution summary.
  \item Add the error-error scatter and geometry-colored contribution view for
  selected datasets where the grouped summary indicates a notable difference.
  \item Keep the full pointwise CSV as a linked audit artifact rather than a
  large table in the report body.
\end{enumerate}

This would make the HTML report more explanatory without turning it into a full
per-point diagnostic atlas.

\section*{Conclusion}

The exact pointwise decomposition makes the dataset-level Truth-RMSE delta
auditable.  It converts the question ``which model won?'' into more precise
questions: which points helped, which hurt, whether those points form geometric
regions, whether a few points dominate, and whether grouped local-dimension
choices explain the result.  For FB01, local-auto is slightly worse overall,
but the result is structured: dimension-one local choices help slightly,
dimension-three choices hurt most in aggregate, and a relatively small number
of points contributes strongly to the final delta.

\section{Proof of the Pointwise Truth-RMSE Delta Decomposition}

Assume
\[
  R_{la}+R_a>0.
\]
If \(R_{la}=R_a=0\), then both models recover the truth perfectly and
\(\Delta_{\mathrm{RMSE}}=0\), so no nontrivial decomposition is needed.

Starting from the definition,
\[
  \Delta_{\mathrm{RMSE}}
  =
  R_{la}-R_a.
\]
Use the elementary identity
\[
  x-y=\frac{x^2-y^2}{x+y},
  \qquad x+y>0.
\]
With \(x=R_{la}\) and \(y=R_a\),
\[
  R_{la}-R_a
  =
  \frac{R_{la}^2-R_a^2}{R_{la}+R_a}.
\]
By definition of Truth RMSE,
\[
  R_{la}^2
  =
  \frac{1}{n}
  \sum_{i=1}^{n}
  (e_i^{la})^2,
  \qquad
  R_a^2
  =
  \frac{1}{n}
  \sum_{i=1}^{n}
  (e_i^a)^2.
\]
Therefore
\[
  R_{la}^2-R_a^2
  =
  \frac{1}{n}
  \sum_{i=1}^{n}
  \left\{
    (e_i^{la})^2-(e_i^a)^2
  \right\}.
\]
Substituting this into the previous display gives
\[
  R_{la}-R_a
  =
  \sum_{i=1}^{n}
  \frac{
    (e_i^{la})^2-(e_i^a)^2
  }{
    n(R_{la}+R_a)
  }.
\]
Thus, if
\[
  c_i
  =
  \frac{
    (e_i^{la})^2-(e_i^a)^2
  }{
    n(R_{la}+R_a)
  },
\]
then
\[
  \Delta_{\mathrm{RMSE}}
  =
  R_{la}-R_a
  =
  \sum_{i=1}^{n} c_i.
\]

\section{Squared-RMSE Alternative}

Sometimes it is simpler to decompose squared RMSE instead of RMSE.  Define
\[
  b_i
  =
  \frac{(e_i^{la})^2-(e_i^a)^2}{n}.
\]
Then
\[
  R_{la}^2-R_a^2
  =
  \sum_{i=1}^{n} b_i.
\]
This squared-error decomposition is more direct algebraically.  The \(c_i\)
decomposition used in the main text is just the squared-error decomposition
rescaled by the positive factor \(R_{la}+R_a\), so that the components sum to
the actual RMSE delta rather than the MSE delta.
